An App for Better Mental Health

Back when I was a high school student, I had average experiences like any other student. The best days were when I was with my friends doing fun stuff together (like hiding in school lockers to scare our teacher!) and then there were the bad days- pressure due to exams and tests, peer pressure, school and homework- particularly got worse as close the dreaded 12th grade came closer, as it was the deciding year that determined one's admission to their dream university. The pressure ramped up like crazy! During this period of my life I saw many of my friends break down due to pressure and stress. Often times I wondered why they wouldn't talk to the school psychiatrist who was there to help us with our mental health, and i wide range of comments such as: not enough time to discuss all the problems, unable to articulate them, stigma attached to mental health, ect. 


Even my own problems had a similar problem, and when I was in school learning UX & UI, for my final project I wanted to make something that could help tackle some of the problems so that the next generation of students don't face such difficult problems in school. 


CONVO is an app that sprung in my mind when I was talking to my teacher for the final project before the program was done. My target audience from the start were high school students with mental health issues. The desired outcome of the app was to help the students deal better with their health issues by creating an app that acted as their personal diary, as well as help them to communicate with their school psychiatrist.


This project was a solo ride, so I was the principle researcher responsible for the research, user flow, wireframes, user interface and experience design. Since I was the only one working on this project, I faced a lot of problems that I had to deal with myself! One of the biggest problems were that I am a beginner and I had to learn a lot of the basic buildings blocks. Apps such as Figma, Sketch, Adobe XD, Invision were alien to me so I spent hours trying to figure how to make an app in the first place! Once I had practiced enough that I was dreaming about making apps in my sleep (this is a true story, by the way) I started seriously designing CONVO. However, I learning and making is a different story. I ended up making 40+ screens before realizing that I wasn't following the proper colour theory, IOS human interface guidelines and worst of all, my app didn't make any sense! I learned the hard way that because I was the designer, the app made sense to me, and might not necessarily make it to other people. So I had to ask for my brother's help, since he is a CTO of a startup company and had interviewed many prospective UX designers that wanted to work in his company, so he had an idea as to where I was going wrong. Not only that, he became a user tester for me as well, and helped me see where I was going wrong. I received many productive comments from him and so I had to begin again, this time I again started from the scratch, with messy handwritten low fidelity wireframes and user flows. However, this time I also studied similar apps and their interface, as well as non similar apps that had the same targeted demographics as me. The result was a newly designed CONVO app that have the same features but a massively different outlook. I also learned to make a more practical user interface design and not just depend on fancy colours, shapes and buttons that made an app look pretty but made it highly impractical. Next, I moved on to researching guidelines and how to make a case study. Though I like researching, UX research was very different compared to academic research. First, my teacher told me the more I have quantitative evidence the better. So, my first draft ended up looking like maths homework. So I had to redo that again and again, till I was satisfied with my research and correct use of their vs there in my case study.

First step in creating the end product.

1

Quick Sketch

I made a sketch of features and screens I wanted in my app at that point, along with the CTAS, logo, and some icons. Then I took a whiteboard and skecthed the basics with a good ol' black marker.





Once I had my problem statements and demographics decided, and had adequate knowledge of designing apps, I began deliberating on how to solve the problem. I had a basic solution in my mind, but to broaden my horizons, I looked through reviews on apps that were dealing with mental health. It gave me some valuable outlook and I ended up incorporating things such as mood tracker and the ability to update one's entry. <br>There were many other suggestions, such as prompts and questions that prompted you to write about your mental health, because though my app focuses on mental health, it isn't an app that solely depends on mood tracking and journal writing, it has an even more integral component to it: The ability of the school psychiatrist to see what you've (mostly) written. More on that later!

Once I had a idea in my mind, I made a quick sketch of it to lay down the basics for my app. The app contained the basics like sign in, sign up screen. Once I was done with those frames, the next one to sketch out was a main screen which would host my main components and the next few screens were the ones the user would see after clicking the main CTAs on the main menu. The question after sketching it out was what my final CTAs would be, as they would be the ones essential to solving the problem that I described in my HMW. However, the CTAs that I sketched out did make it to my final product, albeit in a more different way. I am not surprised because I sketched out those CTAs from my own experiences as a high school student, and how these would have helped me as a student. Therefore, the main CTA was NEW ENTRY was there from the beginning. 

Some things that didn't make it in were the repetitive things like "Rate your feelings" and "Rate your feelings in emoji form".
